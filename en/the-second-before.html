Analysis

The Second Before

There is a clock in football that only FIFA can read. It runs backwards, it runs unevenly, and it stops exactly where it suits.

Miami, quarter-final, 45th minute plus two. Norway’s goalkeeper Örjan Nyland takes a goal kick. The ball climbs, apparently brushes the steel cable of a spider-cam, and comes down on a different line from the one it went up on. Seconds later Jude Bellingham slots it in for 1:1. Norway protest. The French referee Clement Turpin looks at nothing. No monitor, no review, no stoppage.

What arrives instead is remarkable: a statement from FIFA, issued while the match is still being played. The sensor inside the ball, it said, showed no “heartbeat”, and there is “therefore no evidence that the ball touched the cable and altered the movement of the ball”. The world governing body establishes the reality of its own match by press release to its own audience. The man with the whistle is never even asked.

Ten minutes after the break, the counter-test. Norway force the ball over the line from a corner, Torbjörn Heggem, 55th minute, 2:1. Now Turpin walks to the screen. And what he watches is not the goal, not the final duel, not the build-up. He watches a scuffle that took place before the corner had even been taken. Before the ball was in play. Erling Haaland had knocked over Elliot Anderson. Goal cancelled, corner retaken.

Both men were pushing. Men push each other before every corner in every stadium in the world. Who pushed first is a question of slow motion and goodwill. Who fell over is a question of build. The bigger man was penalised, the smaller one was handed a fresh corner.

The video assistant is the only witness who remembers incidents from before the whistle, and forgets the second right before it.

Yet everything hangs on a single condition. The IFAB rulebook permits the video assistant to intervene only in the case of a “clear and obvious error”. Not on questions of interpretation. Not on scuffles. On errors that everybody can see.

What that looks like in practice, German viewers learned on 30 June. Round of 32 near Boston, extra time, 102nd minute: Jonathan Tah scores. The Moroccan referee Jalal Jayed is called to the monitor by video assistant Tatiana Guzman and takes the goal away. Germany go out on penalties. Lutz Wagner, the refereeing analyst on German television, says of the incident: “The goalkeeper has no special rights in the six-yard box.” And: “I do not see a clear offence.”

I do not see a clear offence. That is precisely the threshold below which the video assistant has no business intervening. He intervened anyway.

None of it has consequences, and that is not an accident, it is text. Under the heading “Match validity”, the IFAB itself writes that a match is in principle not invalidated by malfunctions of the video technology, by wrong decisions involving the video assistant, by decisions not to review an incident, or by the review of a non-reviewable situation. Four ways of getting it wrong. Zero consequences. This rulebook does not need interpreting. It needs reading.

Anyone who wants to know how decisions come about inside this organisation need not speculate. On 27 May 2015 a federal court in Brooklyn unsealed an indictment running to 47 counts against 14 defendants. The US Department of Justice spoke of a scheme spanning 24 years and of well over 150 million dollars in bribes and kickbacks. Seven officials were arrested that same morning in a Zurich hotel. Among the matters at issue were the award of a World Cup and the election of a FIFA president. Chuck Blazer, the former general secretary of CONCACAF, had already pleaded guilty.

The same organisation is today selling the turf of the final of 19 July. Cast in acrylic, with a certificate of authenticity, around 390 euros a piece, special editions up to 2,500. Shipping after the final whistle.

The result is not decided yet. The price is.

From the editors A video assistant introduced to correct obvious errors now corrects questions of perception and overlooks steel cables. A governing body that establishes the reality of its own matches by press release has not made refereeing objective. It has made it central.
